Is there such thing as objective meaning?

This question is too terse, so first let’s specify further what we mean by “meaning”. There is meaning as in “the meaning of a sign” or meaning as in “the purpose of your personal life”, “the meaning of humanity” or even “the purpose of living organisms”. Lastly there is also the question of “what is the purpose of the universe?”

David Silver’s Answer on Lex Fridman Podcast #86

A good answer that goes past biological evolution all the way to the arrow of time was given by Deep Mind’s David Silver on Lex Fridman’s podcast. He uses the conceptual model of a goal tree (a concept from AI) to explain how each novel subgoal/sub-purpose is created to help solve the problem of obtaining the higher level “parent” goal. This frames the idea of purpose in terms of ideas from computer science like search and optimization.

One very wide definition of intelligence is the ability to efficiently search the space of instrumental subgoals in order to find the means to accomplish a system’s higher-level goals.

We can conceptualize these instrumental sub goals as “good” shortcuts, “good” biases or “good” constraints that reduce the time and effort to reach the subgoal and more importantly the final goal.

This subgoal view of intelligence seems to be inline with some of the unique qualities of human intelligence. In its modern form human intelligence could probably be distinguished along one line by its significantly higher level of indirection. Humans communicate about objects that are very distant (even absent) from their present and more distant contexts, and this growth in indirectness of representation is also related to the exponential growth of mediating instrumental technology (i.e. subgoals).

Commentary

Speaking Across Nested Concepts
In order to understand such very wide interpretations of the phrase “meaning” or “purpose” and its parent concepts we need to describe something like a scaffolding of senses of the words, each level being more general than the last. This is because the meaning of “purpose” when traced back far enough starts to exceed what most people would consider a valid (normative) interpretation and so we are no longer talking about the same thing.

We usually tend to reserve the use of terms like “meaning” and “purpose” to the living world. Perhaps this is because organisms have the ability to represent their self, environment and its future state whether it be genetic information or other information.

Purpose and Irreversibility
Rather than using a word like “purpose” to talk about the directionality and uniform end state of the 2nd law one could abstract the term at each of the necessary transitions. This will change the topic but it at least transitions the meaning to a subsuming context that can shed light on the included topic. One transition that does have terminology to go with it would be the abiotic to biotic transition (abiogenesis). A pre-transition term would be sometime like “irreversible process” while afterwards we shouldn’t be shy about talking about represented future possible states (i.e., purposes) even for simple bacteria. Of course when speaking more poetically we tend to blur these semantic boundaries.

Gaia

Let me say just a little more on the subject of this abiotic-biotic transition. There are many different strands of people generalizing Darwinian thinking (e.g. neural Darwinism, evolutionary epistemology, genetic algorithms etc.). This general trend goes by the name of Universal Darwinism.

Stability, as a concept is much more general than the survival and reproduction of an organism, but as it subsumes these concepts, it seems to more foundational concept in evolution.

Richard Dawkins in his famous book The Selfish Gene makes this same connection:

Darwin’s ‘survival of the fittest’ is really a special case of a more general law of survival of the stable. The universe is populated by stable things.
— Richard Dawkins, The Selfish Gene (1976)

He goes on later to mention how the stability strategy of genes also includes the cyclic-like quality of being a replicator. Of course this isn’t unique to genes. So stability is the idea but not a simple, static sort of stability, for as Heraclitus says, “No man ever steps in the same river twice”.

That is at geological or cosmic timescales the identity of things is not permanent. Through mutation of a organism’s genetics life can become more stable with minimal change (dynamic stability). In the words of the religious scholar James Carse, the goal isn’t to just win, but to keep the game in play even if it means a slight change in the rules when it is absolutely necessary. (James Carse, Finite and Infinite Games, 1986)

Also with the subset of life that is capable of motion you have another kind of dynamic stability, i.e. movement.

Gaia ⇦ Terra

But where did this “self-preservation” dynamic habit originate from (i.e. abiogenesis)?

Incomplete Nature, a logic of synergy and inversion
It is important to distinguish between three metaphysical modes of dynamics each emergent from the interaction of two or more of the previous modes in a synergistic coupling:

  1. Spontaneous change, i.e., pure chance or randomness (e.g., 2nd law of thermodynamics, energy dissipation)
  2. Self-organization or spontaneous order (e.g. snowflake formation, which as a growth process is self-undermining)
  3. Quasi-cyclic dynamics with self-preservation tendencies (consists of coupling two or more self-organizing systems such that the self-undermining quality of each is constrained by the other and thus you have persistence)

(for more on this recursive logic see Terrence Deacon’s Incomplete Nature: How Mind Emerged from Matter (2011))

Order out of Chaos

So the self-preservation dynamic originates from the spontaneous processes of the universe (e.g. the 2nd law of thermodynamics) via multiple levels of a dialectical emergent logic, and this dissipative dynamic can be viewed as an sort of optimization process at one level and just mechanical dynamics at another.

So from the perspective that assigns a fundamental role to spontaneous change, it seems both the asymmetry or directionality of our intentionality and the inward or circular nature of our self-preservation and reproductive instincts ultimately emerge through a logic of inversion operating on the ground that is the directionality of the 2nd law of thermodynamics, and this law itself relying on the fact that big bang emerged from a singularity.

The process that is life while locally less entropic, sometimes even anti-entropic, is still globally more entropic than the counterfactual of no emergence of life on Earth. So we are spontaneous change seemingly turning around and fighting against itself but which is ultimately and actually helping itself.
