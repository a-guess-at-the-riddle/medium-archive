Some philosophers are monists, arguing that the world must be a unity, one unchanging thing, and that all the multiplicity and change that we see is mere illusion.
Some are dualists, puzzled how the immaterial One (usually Mind or the Ideal) can possibly interact with the material Many (the Body or the World). There are other kinds of dualists, but the idealism/materialism divide has a long history in philosophy under dozens of different names through the ages.
Monists generally reduce the physical world to the ideal, or vice versa, or argued that the ideal and physical worlds were somehow both something else — a “neutral monism.”. But their underlying dualism is inescapable.
Many philosophers prefer triads, triplicities, or trinities as their fundamental structures, and in these we may find the most sensible way to divide the world as we know it into “worlds,” realms, or orders. — Bob Doyle, “One or Many” from informationphilsopher.com

Richard Feynman on The Euclidian vs Babylonian Approach

A good domain to serve as an example is mathematics. In math we have the concept of an axiom which is considered fundamental. The formal and rigorous approach for which the Bourbaki school and “new math” are known is how mathematics is presented in textbooks of the pure math disciplines. But there is an alternative more practical perspective described below by Richard Feynman as “the Babylonian approach” and perhaps associated today with with a philosophy of mathematics known as Quasi-empiricism in mathematics.

There are two kinds of ways of looking at mathematics, which for the purpose of this lecture I will call the Babylonian tradition, and the Greek tradition. In Babylonian schools in mathematics, the student would learn something by doing a large number of examples, until he caught onto the general rule. Also, a large amount of geometry was known — a lot of properties of circles, Theorem of Pythagoras, formulas for the areas of cubes and triangles and everything else — and some degree of argument was available to go from one thing to another. Tables of numerical quantities were available so that you could solve elaborate equations and so on-everything was prepared for calculating things out. But Euclid discovered that there was a way in which all of the theorems of geometry could be ordered from a set of axioms that were particularly simple — and you’re all familiar with that much geometry, I’m sure.
But the Babylonian attitude was — if I make my way of talking what I call Babylonian mathematics — is that you know all these various theorems and many of the connections in between, but you’ve never really realized that it could all come up from a bunch of axioms.
Modern mathematics, the most modern mathematics, concentrates on axioms and demonstrations within a very definite framework of conventions of what’s acceptable and not acceptable as axioms. For example, in geometry, it takes something like Euclid’s axioms (modified to be made more perfect), and then to show the deduction of the system. For instance, it would not be expected that a theorem like Pythagoras’—that the sum of the squares (of the areas of squares) put on the sides of the triangle will equal the area of a square on a hypotenuse—should be an axiom.
On the other hand, from another point of view of geometry—that of Descartes-the Pythagorean Theorem is an axiom. So the first thing we have to worry about is that, even in mathematics, you can start in different places. Because all these various theorems are interconnected by reasoning, there isn’t any real way to say, "Well, these on the bottom, here, are the bottom, and these are connected through logic." Because if you were told this one instead, or this one, you could also run the logic the other way, if you weren’t told that one, and work out that one. It’s like a bridge with lots of members, and it’s overconnected: if pieces have dropped out, you can reconnect it another way.
The mathematical tradition of today is to start with some particular ones, which are chosen by some kind of convention to be axioms, and then to build up the structure from there. The “Babylonian” thing that I’m talking about (which I know is really not Babylonian) is to say, “Well, I happen to know this, and I happen to know that, and maybe I know that, and I work everything out from there. And then tomorrow I forgot that this was true, but I remembered that this was true, and then I reconstruct it again, and so on — I’m never quite sure of where I’m supposed to begin, and where I’m supposed to end; I just remember enough all the time so that as the memory fades, and the pieces fall out, I put this thing back together again every day.
— Richard Feynman, Messenger Lectures. The Character of Physical Law. Lecture 2: The Relation of Mathematics and Physics. [time 24:02]

Nichols of Cusa on the relation of the finite & infinite.

“Therefore, every inquiry proceeds by means of a comparative relation, whether an easy or a difficult one. Hence, the infinite, qua infinite, is unknown; for it escapes all comparative relation. But since comparative relation indicates an agreement in some one respect and, at the same time, indicates an otherness, it cannot be understood independently of number.
Accordingly, number encompasses all things related comparatively. Therefore, number, which is a necessary condition of comparative relation, is present not only in quantity but also in all things which in any manner whatsoever can agree or differ either substantially or accidentally. Perhaps for this reason Pythagoras deemed all things to be constituted and understood through the power of numbers.”
— Nicholas of Cusa, De Docta Ignorantia (On Learned Ignorance)

The Pendulum

Galileo and the Pendulum

He [Galileo] was seventeen and bored listening to the Mass being
celebrated in the cathedral of Pisa. Looking for some object
to arrest his attention, the young medical student began to
focus on a chandelier high above his head, hanging from a
long, thin chain, swinging gently to and fro in the spring
breeze. How long does it take for the oscillations to repeat
themselves, he wondered, timing them with his pulse. To
his astonishment, he found that the lamp took as many
pulse beats to complete a swing when hardly moving at all
as when the wind made it sway more widely. The name of
the perceptive young man, destined to make other momentous
scientific discoveries, was Galileo Galilei.

The legend of how Galileo discovered the isochronism of
the simple pendulum, as told by his biographer Vincenzio
Viviani, is apocryphal, but neither the fact that he found it
nor the profound effects it had on our civilization in later
centuries can be denied.
…
Surprisingly, the physics of the harmonic oscillator — that
is, Galileo’s pendulum — which made it possible to regulate
the flow of time, leads far beyond a mere device for making
accurate clocks. These oscillators have been found to be the
basis not only of what we hear as the sound of music and
see as the colors of light but, via the quantum theory, of
what we understand as the fabric of the universe.

The Integration of All Things

A Fractal Earth

In modeling complexity, population density is perhaps one of the best variables that one can track. This is because of the role it plays in the phrase transition between whether the dominant dynamical mode is one of additive dynamics or multiplicative dynamics.

Lets perform a though experiment. That is, imagine early humans all living in isolated primitive small groups. The difference in value creation between each tribe would be like flipping coins where each flip is entirely independent of the next. In such an environment we probably wouldn’t have as much simultaneous differentiation (specialization) and integration (organizations) which come from the network of interactions of our modern social organization. These interactions have a multiplicative effect on our ability to generate value (feedback). In recent times these networks are becoming huge and very stratified. It is like globalization (speaking very generally) is leading towards the globe becoming a literal fractal.

Many of the more equally distributed characteristics were (and are still) adapted to the population density (and thus very small social networks) of our ancestral environment (tribes). But in the modern technological era cities, regions, nations, and the globe itself are becoming integrated networks with distribution networks of information and goods. This means cultural information is becoming more unequal (e.g., pareto dist.) while at least some of our genetic information like IQ remains normally distributed. A product of the fact that cultural change is outpacing genetic change.

But the network metaphor isn’t really entirely accurate (if you think of it as flat that is) as certain points in these networks are more valuable than others. Since information itself has value (an interesting twist on the fact/value dichotomy) your access to the value generation pathways can become very important. Even if you wanted to try and become a spontaneous generator (a source node) of valuable information you would need to first access already or maybe soon to be valuable information to help guide you, and in a dynamic and changing world the really valuable information is the information that nobody knows is valuable yet (just like investing, i.e. “get in early”). It might not even be very predictable.

Related to Peterson’s use of the edge of chaos metaphor, it is interesting that at least in the logistic map (the archetypical nonlinear map for period doubling to chaos behavior) which was first used as toy model of population dynamics, the single parameter value at which the period doubling (i.e. ordered) behavior undergoes a phase change into chaotic dynamics is known as the edge of chaos and is a fractal, specifically a Cantor set. Since it is a fractal it has a power law distribution. But how can humanity develop over time into such a small point out of a larger set?

Well there is a model property called self-organized criticality. From Wikipedia:

Self-organized criticality (SOC) is a property of dynamical systems that have a critical point as an attractor. Their macroscopic behavior thus displays the spatial or temporal scale-invariance characteristic of the critical point of a phase transition, but without the need to tune control parameters to a precise value, because the system, effectively, tunes itself as it evolves towards criticality.

Imagine you somehow were able to magically force every thought and every habit in your mind to be equally rewarded with the relevant neurotransmitters like dopamine no matter the actions you take in any context. You would not become any more efficient or adapted and actual start slide backwards.

So people respond to incentives just like neurons respond to dopamine. And just like some neural circuits are much more valuable than others some people are much more valuable than others.

Skills and abilities are not distributed equally at all. In fact we will probably come to learn more and more that highly unequal distributions like we have in personal productivity are necessary for efficient information processing. We have to take advantage and reward those productive agents to the extent that it incentivizes them to be productive and doesn't cause the rest to revolt.

Highly unequal value hierarchies are ubiquitous in the world of complex phenomena. Think of how the distribution of words used in texts is wildly skewed (Zipf’s law) or how memory hierarchies in computers are designed to be farther and farther away from the CPU and larger and larger (cpu registers, caches, volatile memory, nonvolatile memory).

Much more can be said with this information processing conceptual lens too. Computers of the future will probably look more and more like capitalism in that they will be more like the brain (i.e. self-organizing).

There is much more to be said here but hopefully you can gloss from this that it is quite intuitive why communism would just not work.
